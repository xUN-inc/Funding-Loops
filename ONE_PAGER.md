# Follow The Money — One Pager

**AI For Accountability Hackathon · Demo April 29, 2026**

> An interactive investigation console that surfaces circular funding loops between Canadian charities, names the people sitting on multiple boards inside each loop, and writes a publishable forensic memo in plain English — all from public CRA T3010 filings.

---

## The problem

Canadian charities legitimately move billions to each other through gifts to qualified donees. But buried inside that flow are **circular patterns** — money that leaves Charity A, lands at B, and comes back to A — sometimes routed through people who sit on both boards. These cycles can be:

- **Structural** (a federated parent / chapter network — normal).
- **Receipt generation** (donations bounced to inflate donor receipts).
- **Revenue inflation** (the same dollar counted as revenue at every stop).
- **Overhead extraction** (most of the money never reaches a program).

The CRA publishes the data, but no public tool ranks the loops, classifies them, or tells a journalist *who* is in control.

## What we built

A three-panel investigation console:

1. **Loop list** — every detected cycle, ranked by total dollar flow, with a one-glance risk chip and the worst-case classification of any participant.
2. **Network graph** — the loop drawn as a force-directed graph. Node size = revenue, ring color = classification severity, arrows = direction of money. Hover for the org's overhead, gov-funding share, classification.
3. **Forensic memo** — three plain-English paragraphs generated by Gemini 2.5 Flash, naming the classification of each org, naming the directors who sit on multiple boards inside the cycle, and ending with a verdict: **LIKELY BENIGN**, **REQUIRES SCRUTINY**, or **RED FLAG**.

A second view, **Public Recipients**, applies the same accountability lens to companies, NGOs, universities, and individuals receiving federal grants or Alberta procurement — closing the "feels charity-only" gap.

## Why it matters

A journalist or auditor with no SQL skills can:

- See the largest suspicious cycles in seconds.
- Read a quotable summary that **names individuals** sitting on ≥ 2 boards inside one cycle (the strongest single-decision-maker signal).
- Cross-reference whether **federal grant money** is entering that same cycle — turning a shell-game suspicion into a story.
- Export to CSV for further work.

## How to demo it (60 seconds)

1. `npm run old` → open `http://localhost:3000/old.html`.
2. The largest loop auto-selects with a pre-generated memo (zero wait).
3. Click ⚙ Settings — bump the initial fetch to 100, sort by **shared directors desc**, filter to **RED FLAG only**. Apply.
4. Pick any 2-node back-and-forth loop — point at the two arrows curving in opposite directions, one for each direction of flow.
5. Read the memo aloud. The verdict, the classification labels, and the named directors are all factual — there is no embellishment.

## Stack at a glance

- **Backend:** Express + node-postgres, in-process caches (5-min TTL).
- **Database:** PostgreSQL with `cra`, `fed`, `ab`, `general` schemas (read-only).
- **AI:** Google Gemini 2.5 Flash for memos and the executive narrative. Token usage logged per call.
- **Frontend:** Two interchangeable UIs — a vanilla-JS three-panel console (`old.html`) and a Vite + React 18 SPA (`index.html`) sharing the same API.
- **Visualization:** D3.js v7 force-directed graph, with curved bidirectional links and label haloes for clarity in tight cycles.

## What we explicitly do NOT do

- We do **not** re-detect loops — those come from the existing `CRA/scripts/advanced/` pipeline.
- We do **not** write back to the database — purely a read-only investigation surface.
- We do **not** pull external data, news, or social signals — every claim is grounded in the CRA filing.
